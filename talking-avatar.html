Four Activities

Choose a Lesson

Each activity uses the same Talkr app workflow but applies it to a different theme — from literary characters to art history to historical figures.

1st – 2nd Class

Create a Character Profile: Roald Dahl's Willy Wonka

Bring a character from Charlie and the Chocolate Factory to life by creating a digital talking portrait and recording their voice.

Students explore the character of Willy Wonka by creating a visual and narrative character profile. They design a portrait incorporating his unique traits and whimsical personality, then bring it to life using the Talkr app to animate and record a voiceover. The lesson combines literacy, visual arts, and digital creativity — allowing students to delve into character development and storytelling in an engaging way.

Learning Outcomes

1

Creative Expression: Students produce a personalised portrait of Willy Wonka, showcasing his eccentric and imaginative traits.

2

Literary Understanding: Students demonstrate understanding of Willy Wonka's character and role in the story.

3

Digital Literacy: Students use animation and voice recording tools to bring their portrayal to life.

4

Communication Skills: Students craft and share a narrative voiceover that reflects Willy Wonka's personality.

5

Reflective Learning: Students reflect on how their artistic and narrative choices capture the essence of the character.

3rd – 4th Class

Create an Artist Profile: The Movement of Fauvism

Using the Talkr app, create a digital character profile of a Fauvist artist, combining their artistic style and personal story.

Students explore the bold and expressive art movement of Fauvism by creating a fictional artist inspired by its principles. They design a portrait using exaggerated colours and simplified forms, reflecting Fauvist techniques. Using the Talkr app, students animate their artist and record a voiceover to express their character's artistic philosophy. The lesson integrates art history, creative expression, and digital technology.

Learning Outcomes

1

Creative Expression: Students produce a vivid Fauvist-inspired portrait that captures the essence of the movement's style.

2

Art Historical Understanding: Students demonstrate knowledge of Fauvism, including bold colours, simplified forms, and emotional expression.

3

Digital Literacy: Students use animation and voice recording to bring their Fauvist artist to life.

4

Communication Skills: Students articulate their artist's backstory and philosophy through a voiceover presentation.

5

Reflective Learning: Students reflect on their artistic choices and the emotional impact of Fauvist techniques.

3rd – 4th Class

Create a Titanic Passenger Profile

Create a digital talking portrait of an imaginary Titanic passenger, bringing their story to life.

Students create a character profile of a fictional Titanic passenger, combining historical context with creative storytelling. Using digital tools, they design a portrait of their character — considering class, background, and motivations for travelling. Students then animate their character using the Talkr app, recording a voiceover to bring them to life. The lesson merges art, history, and technology.

Learning Outcomes

1

Creative Expression: Students create a personalised Titanic character portrait reflecting their understanding of the era.

2

Historical Understanding: Students demonstrate knowledge of the Titanic's social structure, purpose, and historical significance.

3

Digital Literacy: Students use digital drawing and animation tools to design and animate their character.

4

Communication Skills: Students craft and share a backstory through a recorded voiceover, enhancing narrative and oral expression.

5

Reflective Learning: Students connect their work to historical themes, reflecting on the experiences of Titanic passengers.

5th – 6th Class

Create a Viking Warrior Profile

Bring a Viking warrior character to life by creating a digital talking portrait and sharing their story.

Students create a character profile of a Viking warrior, explorer, or craftsperson, combining historical understanding with artistic expression. They design and paint a portrait of their Viking using traditional art materials, then bring the character to life using the Talkr app to animate and record a voiceover. Students explore the daily life, culture, and values of the Vikings while developing storytelling and digital literacy skills.

Learning Outcomes

1

Creative Expression: Students produce a unique Viking character portrait, showcasing bold artistic choices.

2

Historical Understanding: Students demonstrate knowledge of Viking roles, culture, and motivations through their character's design and backstory.

3

Digital Literacy: Students animate and voice their Viking character, combining traditional art with modern technology.

4

Communication Skills: Students present their character to peers, articulating their character's story and cultural significance.

5

Reflective Learning: Students reflect on their creative and historical choices, connecting art and history meaningfully.

The Activity Workflow

Five Steps — Every Activity

The same five-step process works across all four activities — students simply apply it to a different character or context.

01

Choose Your Character

Pick a character, historical figure, or artist to profile. Consider their personality, background, and what makes them unique.

02

Design the Portrait

Draw or create a digital image of your character — physical art materials, drawing apps like Procreate, or a combination of both.

03

Set Up the Avatar

Import your portrait into the Talkr app. Set up the animation features — eyes, mouth, and facial movement — ready for recording.

04

Record the Voiceover

Write a short script and record it in character. What would this person say about themselves, their world, or their story?

05

Share Your Work

Play your talking portrait for the class. Engage in peer feedback, gallery walks, or a showcase presentation.

Classroom Discussion

Key Thinking Questions

Sample questions to use across the activities — adapt for your chosen character or theme.

Higher-Order Thinking

  • Why do you think this character acts the way they do? What shaped them?
  • If your character could give advice to someone today, what would they say?
  • How might your character's experiences have changed them over time?
  • What emotions did you try to show through your artistic choices?
  • How could art and technology help us understand historical figures or fictional characters?
  • If you could meet your character, what would you ask them?
  • How might your character's story be different if they lived in today's world?

Lower-Order Thinking

  • Who is your character? What do they look like?
  • What is their name and where do they come from?
  • What do they do? What is their job or role?
  • What colours did you use and why?
  • What does your character say in their voiceover?
  • What materials did you use to create your portrait?
  • What did you enjoy most about this activity?

Assessment

💬

Structured gallery walks, group rotations, or spotlight presentations allow students to give and receive constructive feedback on creativity and presentation.

🔍

Students reflect on their artistic and narrative choices — what worked, what they'd do differently, and how their work connects to the theme or context.

What You'll Need

Resources & Materials

Materials vary by activity — physical art materials for some, purely digital for others. Here's a general guide.

Art Materials

  • Paper: A3 or A4 drawing paper, sketchbooks, or cardstock
  • Sketching: Pencils, erasers, and sharpeners for initial work
  • Colour: Coloured pencils, markers, watercolours, acrylic or poster paints
  • Chalk Pastels: Bold, bright soft pastels — ideal for Fauvism activity
  • Collage (optional): Coloured paper, scissors, glue sticks, stickers
  • Markers: Fine-tip black markers for outlines and details
  • Reference images: Visual examples relevant to each activity's theme

Digital Tools & Hardware

  • iPads or Tablets: One per student or per pair — fully charged
  • Talkr App: Pre-installed and updated on all devices
  • Headphones with microphones: For recording voiceovers
  • Styluses (optional): For more precise digital drawing
  • Classroom Wi-Fi: Stable connection for app use and references
  • Drawing apps (optional): Procreate, Tayasui Sketches, or Canva
  • Projector / Interactive whiteboard: For teacher demonstrations

Inclusive Teaching

Differentiation Strategies

All four activities are designed to be fully inclusive. Here's how to adapt for different learners.

Content

Vary the Resources

Offer simplified summaries, illustrated timelines, or video clips for students needing extra support. Provide in-depth texts, primary sources, or extended research prompts for advanced learners.

Process

Scaffold the Steps

Use character profile templates with sentence starters for less confident students. Offer open-ended prompts for advanced learners. Pair students for peer support — one focuses on drawing, the other on writing.

Product

Flexible Output

Less confident students: a simple portrait and a short structured script. Advanced students: a detailed portrait with an elaborate backstory and longer voiceover. All outputs are valued equally.

Environment

Station-Based Support

Set up a guided station with teacher support, an independent station for confident learners, and a tech help station for the app. Use assistive technology — text-to-speech tools, voice typing — where needed.

Assessment

Individual Progress

Assess based on effort, creativity, and engagement — not uniform standards. For less confident learners, ask simple reflection questions. For advanced learners, encourage deeper critical reflection.

Extension

Going Further

Challenge advanced students to design a new invention (Wonka), create a philosophical motto (Fauvism), add dramatic storytelling elements (Titanic), or include symbolic Viking patterns with a detailed backstory.
